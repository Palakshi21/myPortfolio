Bridges & Bytes

OpenAI's ChatGPT launched in November of 2022 - halfway through the second year of my Bachelor of Engineering, at a point when I'd already gotten used to pen-and-paper problem solving and consulting classic resources like classmates and StackOverflow. It was an entertaining text model, something that just about sounded like a person, but entirely unhelpful if I wanted to solve my circuits homework. At best, it was a time-efficient method to debug syntax errors and even then, there was a natural suspicion of this tool's correctness.

In the following months, there was a warmer and more lenient approach to using AI chat agents. You could feel it in students asking more questions, not to see if the AI matched their own answer, but as a call to the void to seek an answer in the first place. A faster Google search, a custom grammer editor, a quick paraphraser. With greater scientific accuracy, more human-sounding language and a somewhat earned confidence in its abilities - AI tools became an obvious aid for students. An initial, panicky response to this shifting online exams to paper and a blanket ban on AI inputs in assignments. The equivelant of sticking your finger in a dyke wall thinking it's going to hold back the sea forever.
